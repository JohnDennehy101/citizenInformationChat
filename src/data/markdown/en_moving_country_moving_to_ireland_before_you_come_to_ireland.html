Ireland ](/en/moving-country/moving-to-ireland/) > Before you come to Ireland # Before you come to Ireland * Introduction * Visas and permission to enter Ireland * Coming to Ireland to work * Coming to Ireland to study * General information about living in Ireland ## Introduction Before you come to Ireland, you need to know if you are entitled to live here. Your right to live in Ireland depends mainly on your nationality and what you intend to do when you come here. ## Visas and permission to enter Ireland There are some general rules which apply regardless of the reason why you are coming to Ireland. **UK nationals** : People who are citizens of the United Kingdom (UK) are [ entitled to live in Ireland ](/en/government-in-ireland/ireland-and-the- uk/residence-rules-UK-citizens/) without any conditions or restrictions. **EEA and Switzerland:** In general, [ nationals of EEA countries or Switzerland, ](/en/moving-country/moving-to-ireland/rights-of-residence-in- ireland/residence-rights-eu-national/) have the right to enter Ireland. You do not need a visa but you will need a valid passport or identity card in order to land. You are generally entitled to live in Ireland if you are employed or self-employed. You can to come here to study or retire here if you meet certain conditions. Your family can come to Ireland too, but they may need a [ visa to enter Ireland ](/en/moving-country/visas-for-ireland/visa-requirements-for-entering- ireland/) if they are not EEA or Swiss citizens themselves. **Other countries:** Citizens of other countries may need to apply for a visa. If you need a visa or not, you will still need [ permission to enter in Ireland ](/en/moving-country/visas-for-ireland/permission-to-land-in-ireland/) and [ permission to remain here ](/en/moving-country/moving-to-ireland/rights- of-residence-in-ireland/residence-rights-of-non-eea-nationals-in-ireland/) . ## Coming to Ireland to work You may need an employment permit to work in Ireland, or permission to open a business, if you are not in one of the categories below: **UK citizens** can live and work in Ireland without restriction. If you have family members who are from outside the UK, EEA or Switzerland, they must apply to join you in Ireland. **EEA and Swiss citizens** have the right to work or set up a business in Ireland **.** You are entitled to be treated in the same way as Irish workers. You can have [ family members ](/en/moving-country/moving-to-ireland/rights- of-residence-in-ireland/residence-rights-eu-national/) come with you to Ireland. Younger people from certain countries outside the UK, EEA or Switzerland can apply to come to Ireland on a [ working holiday ](/en/moving-country/working- in-ireland/employment-permits/working-holiday-visa-in-Ireland/) . ## Coming to Ireland to study You may need to apply for a [ student visa ](/en/moving-country/visas-for- ireland/student-visas/) before you come to Ireland to study. If you donâ€™t need a visa, you still need [ permission to enter ](/en/moving-country/visas-for- ireland/permission-to-land-in-ireland/) Ireland. This means you must show that you satisfy the [ conditions to study in Ireland ](/en/moving-country/moving- to-ireland/studying-in-ireland/immigration-nonEEA-students/) . Different rules apply for [ short-term courses ](https://www.irishimmigration.ie/coming-to- study-in-ireland/what-are-my-study-visa-options/how-to-apply-for-a-short-c- visit-study-visa/) . ## General information about living in Ireland **Returning to Ireland:** Our section on returning to Ireland has information about what you should know if you are planning on returning to Ireland after living abroad. **Retiring in Ireland** : Find out about [ returning to Ireland to retire ](/en/returning-to-ireland/retiring-to-ireland/retiring-to-ireland-as-a- returning-irish-emigrant/) , or [ moving to Ireland to retire ](/en/moving- country/moving-to-ireland/coming-to-live-in-ireland/retiring-to-ireland/) . **Government:** Ireland is a parliamentary democracy. All residents have the [ right to vote ](/en/government-in-ireland/elections-and- referenda/voting/right-to-vote/) in [ local elections ](/government_in_ireland/elections_and_referenda/local_elections/) and EU citizens also have the right to vote in [ European elections ](/government_in_ireland/elections_and_referenda/european_elections/) . UK citizens have the right to vote in [ national elections ](/en/government-in- ireland/elections-and-referenda/types-of-elections-and-referendums/) as well. Read further information about the [ national government ](/en/government-in- ireland/how-government-works/) and [ local government ](/en/government-in- ireland/how-government-works/local-and-regional-government/functions-of-local- authorities/) . **Tax and social insurance contributions:** If you are working or self- employed you must pay [ PRSI contributions ](/en/social-welfare/irish-social- welfare-system/social-insurance-prsi/social-insurance/) in the same way as Irish people. If you are resident in Ireland, you may be liable for [ income tax ](/en/money-and-tax/tax/income-tax/) and for [ capital taxes. ](/en/money- and-tax/tax/capital-taxes/) There are specific rules about [ residency for tax purposes ](/en/money-and-tax/tax/moving-country-and-taxation/tax-residence- and-domicile-in-ireland/) . **Education:** Free [ primary education ](/en/education/primary-and-post- primary-education/going-to-primary-school/) and [ secondary education ](/en/education/primary-and-post-primary-education/going-to-post-primary- school/) is available to all children aged under 18 legally resident in Ireland. There are also some fee-paying primary and secondary schools available. School attendance is mandatory until the age 16. **Equality:** Ireland has equality legislation that prohibits discrimination in [ employment ](/en/employment/equality-in-work/equality-in-the-workplace/) and [ access to services ](https://www.ihrec.ie/guides-and-tools/human-rights- and-equality-in-the-provision-of-good-and-services/what-does-the-law- say/equal-status-acts/) on a number of grounds including nationality and race. **Housing:** Some people live in [ owner-occupied private housing ](/en/housing/owning-a-home/) while a number live in [ private rented accommodation ](/en/housing/renting-a-home/) . [ Social ](/en/housing/local- authority-and-social-housing/) housing is available, but you generally need to have been resident in Ireland for a period of time before you become eligible for it. **Health services:** If you are ordinarily resident in Ireland, you may be entitled to a range of [ health services ](/en/moving-country/moving-to- ireland/introduction-to-the-irish-system/health-care-in-ireland/) that are either free of charge or subsidised by the Government. There is also a range of private health care services. Your permission to stay in Ireland may include a requirement that you have [ private health insurance. ](/en/health/health-system/private-health-insurance/) **Social security entitlements:** If you are moving to Ireland you need to know about your social security entitlements. EEA and Swiss nationals come under the general rules on [ social security for migrant workers ](/en/social- welfare/irish-social-welfare-system/claiming-a-social-welfare-payment/social- insurance-contributions-from-abroad/) . There are [ social security arrangements ](https://www.gov.ie/en/publication/dc7910-guidelines-on-application-of- bilateral-agreements/) with a small number of other countries. These generally allow contributions in the other countries to be combined with your Irish contributions to enable you to qualify for benefits. **Social assistance payments:** Everyone, including Irish citizens, has to meet the [ habitual residence requirement ](/en/social-welfare/irish-social- welfare-system/social-assistance-payments/habitual-residence-condition/) if applying for a social assistance payment. Different rules apply to [ EEA citizens ](/en/moving-country/working-in-ireland/migrant-workers/eu-citizens- and-social-welfare/) . **Pets:** There are regulations about importing pets from abroad, so you need find out about the [ procedures for bringing your pet to Ireland. ](/en/moving-country/moving-to-ireland/coming-to-live-in-ireland/bringing- pets-to-ireland/) **Customs:** There are [ customs regulations about the importation of prohibited or restricted goods ](/en/travel-and-recreation/travel-to- ireland/customs-regulations-for-travellers/) and [ Customs and Excise officers have the power to carry out searches of the baggage of people travelling to Ireland ](/en/travel-and-recreation/travel-to-ireland/examination-of- travellers-baggage-by-customs-and-excise-officers/) . **Driving, cars and transport:** Full driving licences from all other EU member states (and some other countries) are recognised for use in Ireland. You should find out if you need to [ convert your driving licence to an Irish one ](/en/travel-and-recreation/motoring/driver-licensing/exchanging-foreign- driving-permit/) . If you want to bring your car to Ireland you need to know about [ importing a car ](/en/moving-country/moving-to-ireland/coming-to-live- in-ireland/importing-car-into-ireland/) and implications for Vehicle Registration Tax (VRT). ### Further information You can get further information from your nearest [ Irish embassy or consulate ](https://www.ireland.ie/en/dfa/embassies/) or the [ embassy or consulate of your country in Ireland ](https://www.ireland.ie/en/dfa/embassies-in-ireland/) . _**Page edited:** 8 November 2023 _ [
