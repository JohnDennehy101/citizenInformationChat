**English** | Gaeilge **You are here:** [ Home ](/en/) > [ Employment ](/en/employment/) > [ Employment rights and conditions ](/en/employment/employment-rights-and- conditions/) > [ Pay and employment ](/en/employment/employment-rights-and- conditions/pay-and-employment/) > Case study: Minimum wage # Case study: Minimum wage The [ national minimum wage ](/en/employment/employment-rights-and- conditions/pay-and-employment/minimum-wage/) increased to €12.70 from 1 January 2024. Sean works 39 hours per week including 3 hours on a Saturday. He is entitled to the minimum wage of €12.70 as he is 20 years of age, (People aged 20 and above are entitled to 100% of the minimum wage) an employee and isn't on a training or study course. He is paid a gross wage of €500 per week. Sean's pay includes a €20 premium for working Saturdays. He asked his employer about the national minimum wage and his employer gave him the following information: * The pay reference period is weekly * Dividing the gross pay (€500) by the hours worked (39), this gives an average hourly pay of €12.82 This is above the current national minimum wage of €12.70 per hour. Is Sean's employer correct? ### **Answer** No, Sean's employer is incorrect. The €20 premium for working on Saturdays should not be included for national minimum wage purposes. This means that only €480 should be divided by 39, giving an average hourly pay of €12.30 - below the national minimum wage of €12.70.
